Product Design

By combining empathy, creativity and rationality, I aim to understand and solve problems in a human centered way, such that the resulting solutions are always useful, usable and desirable. Below is an overview and some examples of how I apply design thinking to building software.

Design Thinking

At the highest level, a design driven processes can be broken into three broad phases: developing an understanding of the problem, exploring solution concepts and identifying which solutions address the problem best.

Understanding

The better we understand the problem, the people with the problem and the situation in which they experience the problem, the more capable we are of building powerful solutions.

A product design process that internalizes the power of understanding is not only a core driver of innovation but also a defensible business strategy. Aesthetics and functionalities can always be copied but the insights and perspectives that lead to great products are nearly impossible to replicate.

Exploration

Sometimes your first idea might be the best solution, but isn’t never the only possible one. With creative exercises teams can leverage their insights and understandings to generate many solution concepts and uncover opportunities that may have otherwise never been discovered.

If we don’t go beyond what we already know, into the unknown, we limit our ability to discover radically new opportunities.

Determining Fitness

Through qualitative and quantitative testing we can determine which ideas work best and confidently focus on refining and implementing the most appropriate solutions.

Every design challenge is unique and as a result a strong design processes needs to be able to adapt as things change. Being able to identifying when a tactic or strategy isn’t working and then taking a different approach, is central to facilitating this adaptability.

Visual Design

The true beauty of a product is in how it empowers its users.

Visual design is first and foremost about affordances, communicating through it’s form how it works and how to engage with it. Appealing aesthetics alone will never result in a great product. A product must first be useful, useable and desirable before it can be truly beautiful.
